"The Laugh of the Medusa," published in 1975 by French writer and philosopher Hélène Cixous, is a classic feminist essay that first systematically elaborates the theory of "écriture féminine" (feminine writing). This passionate and poetic text not only critiques patriarchal language's repression of women but also calls on women to reclaim their bodies and voices through writing, remaining one of the most influential texts of French feminist theory.

Cixous's opening manifesto still resonates powerfully today: "Woman must write herself, must write about women, must bring women to writing." She notes that women have been violently expelled from writing just as they have been expelled from their bodies—for the same reasons, by the same laws, with the same fatal goals. Cixous argues that writing is the key path for women to reclaim subjectivity, the fundamental way to put themselves into text, into the world, into history.

"Écriture féminine" is the central concept of this essay. Cixous argues that women's writing should be different from men's because women "speak with the body." She opposes the binary oppositions, linear logic, and hierarchical structures of patriarchal language, advocating instead a fluid, multiple, open mode of writing—a writing that originates from the body, respects difference, and embraces contradiction. Drawing on Derrida's deconstruction theory while combining it with women's experience, Cixous creates a unique feminist poetics.

The metaphor of Medusa is the essay's most striking element. In Greek mythology, Medusa is a terrifying monster whose gaze can turn people to stone, ultimately beheaded by the hero Perseus. Cixous reinterprets this myth, noting that Medusa was demonized because she represented a feminine power that threatened patriarchal order. Cixous declares: "Medusa, she is not ugly, she is not deadly, she is beautiful, she is laughing." Through this reinterpretation, Cixous transforms female creativity from a repressed "other" into an affirmative, life-affirming force.

Cixous's critique of patriarchal language is profound and incisive. She points out that Western thought is built on the "systematic repression of women's experience," that patriarchal language not only excludes women's voices but constructs a symbolic order that defines women as "lack," "other," "object." Drawing on Lacanian psychoanalytic theory, Cixous notes that women are excluded from the symbolic order, but this marginal position grants them the possibility of subverting that order.

The essay's emphasis on the relationship between body and writing is groundbreaking. Cixous argues that feminine writing must "return to the body"—to the body that has been confiscated, alienated, displayed as an "uncanny stranger." She describes the multiple, fluid, non-linear qualities of the female body, arguing that these qualities should be expressed in writing. Cixous's description of female sexuality—as multiple, diffuse, non-phallocentric—challenges Freud and Lacan's penis envy theory, providing new language for feminist sexual politics.

Cixous's reinterpretation of the concept of "bisexuality" is also noteworthy. She opposes views that understand bisexuality as simply adding masculine and feminine traits, advocating instead for a more fluid, non-essentialist conception of gender. Cixous argues that everyone carries multiple genders within themselves, and one of the tasks of feminine writing is to liberate these repressed gender possibilities.

The essay's influence on literary criticism has been profound. Cixous's theory has inspired extensive research on women's writing styles, themes, and strategies, advancing the development of feminist literary criticism. Her emphasis on "body writing" influenced subsequent feminist performance art and body art; her attention to the relationship between language and politics provided resources for feminist discourse analysis; her reinterpretation of myth and narrative inspired feminist mythological studies.

However, Cixous's theory has also faced criticism. Scholars have noted that her essentialist tendency regarding the category "woman"—defining certain qualities (such as fluidity, multiplicity, bodiliness) as "feminine"—may reinforce gender stereotypes. Some critics argue that her writing style is too abstract and elitist to be accessible to ordinary readers. Others point out her insufficient attention to race, class, and colonialism, primarily reflecting the experience of white, middle-class European women.

From a contemporary perspective, some of Cixous's views may require updating. Her emphasis on biological sex may create tension with contemporary transgender theory; her discourse on "female essence" may be overly deterministic; her idealization of the pre-Oedipal phase may overlook power relations within it. However, these limitations should not obscure Cixous's groundbreaking contribution—she redefined writing as political action, reintroduced the body into theoretical discourse, and provided imaginative language for feminist literature and politics.

Cixous's legacy lies in her demonstration of how writing can become a tool of liberation. She proved that language is not a neutral medium but a site of power operation; she showed how women can challenge patriarchal order by creating new forms of expression. In this sense, "The Laugh of the Medusa" is not merely a theoretical essay but a call to action—it invites every woman to pick up the pen, write her story, and make her voice heard.
