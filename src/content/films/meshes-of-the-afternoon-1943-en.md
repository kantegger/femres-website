"Meshes of the Afternoon," created by American experimental film pioneer Maya Deren in 1943, is a 14-minute short that stands as both a foundational work of the American avant-garde film movement and an important milestone in feminist cinema history. Using dreamlike visual language to explore female subconscious, identity, and gender power relations, its innovative film grammar and profound psychological insight remain stunning today.

The film's narrative follows dream logic rather than real-world logic. A woman (played by Deren herself) falls asleep in her home one afternoon and enters a dream world filled with symbols and repetitions. In the dream, she repeatedly chases a mysterious hooded figure, undergoes multiple identity transformations, and ultimately meets a violent end. Deren employs circular narrative, multiple selves, and symbolic objects to create a hypnotic yet unsettling viewing experience.

The film's symbolic system is rich and complex. Recurring objects—the key, knife, flower, telephone, and phonograph—all carry multiple meanings. The key symbolizes entry and enclosure, the knife symbolizes violence and castration anxiety, the flower symbolizes fragility and femininity, while the telephone and phonograph symbolize interrupted and distorted communication. Deren refuses to provide single, definitive interpretations, instead inviting viewers into the polysemous space of dreams.

Deren's representation of "multiple selves" is the film's most innovative aspect. In the dream, the female protagonist encounters "another self" multiple times—sometimes outside the window, sometimes in bed, sometimes in the mirror. These multiple selves are both observers and observed, both subject and object. Through this technique, Deren explores the split and fluid nature of female identity, anticipating later feminist theory's deconstruction of the "unified subject."

The film's presentation of male imagery is profoundly critical. The mysterious hooded figure never reveals their face, and their gender remains ambiguous—possibly male, possibly female. However, the film's ending reveals that this figure has some connection to the protagonist's husband (played by Deren's then-husband Alexander Hammid). This revelation suggests that the fears and threats in the dream may originate from real-life heterosexual relationships. Through this structure, Deren critiques the continuous, potential violence that women experience under patriarchy.

Deren's film language thoroughly breaks from traditional Hollywood narrative norms. She employs various experimental techniques: slow motion, dissolves, reverse playback, discontinuous editing, subjective camera angles, and more. Camera positions are often at unconventional heights—Deren explained that this was because she herself was quite short, so the camera naturally assumed lower angles. This "female perspective" creates a viewing experience different from mainstream cinema, challenging the hegemony of the male gaze.

The film's treatment of space is also noteworthy. The story takes place primarily in an ordinary suburban home—the living room, bedroom, kitchen, and stairs. Deren transforms these everyday spaces into psychologically charged dream landscapes. Particularly the recurring elements of windows, doors, and corridors create a labyrinthine sense of space, symbolizing the complexity and enclosure of women's inner worlds.

"Meshes of the Afternoon" has had a profound influence on subsequent feminist and experimental cinema. Deren pioneered women's autonomous film production—she wrote, directed, and acted in the film, and participated in editing and post-production. She proved that women do not need to rely on the mainstream film industry and can independently create works with artistic value and political significance. The film's attention to women's psychology, dreams, and bodily experience provided an important paradigm for later feminist cinema.

Deren's theoretical writings complement her film practice. In numerous essays, she elaborated her film concepts, arguing that cinema should break free from the narrative constraints of literature and explore the unique possibilities of vision and time. She paid particular attention to the concept of the "camera eye"—the camera is not merely a recording tool but a tool for creating meaning. These theoretical viewpoints had significant influence on later experimental film and feminist film theory.

From a contemporary perspective, "Meshes of the Afternoon" still possesses powerful artistic force and political significance. The film's exploration of female subconscious, identity splitting, and gender violence forms a dialogue with contemporary feminist theory. Deren's experimental spirit—her brave innovation in film language, her faithful expression of women's experience, her resolute challenge to mainstream norms—continues to inspire film creators today.

Maya Deren's legacy lies in her demonstration of how cinema can become a tool for women's self-expression and self-exploration. "Meshes of the Afternoon" is not only a film about women's dreams but a film created by a woman, from a woman's perspective, exploring female subjectivity. In this sense, it transcends its era to become an eternal classic in feminist cinema history.
