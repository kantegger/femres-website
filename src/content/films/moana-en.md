Ron Clements and John Musker’s *Moana* (2016) represents a seismic shift in the Disney Princess canon, effectively replacing the "royal-in-waiting" narrative with a rigorous exploration of indigenous leadership and environmental stewardship. Set in ancient Polynesia, the film follows Moana (Auli'i Cravalho), the daughter of a village chief who is chosen by the ocean to restore the heart of Te Fiti—a life-giving goddess whose absence has triggered an ecological blight. The film is historically significant for being Disney’s first "princess" feature to completely omit a romantic subplot. Instead, it centers Moana’s journey on the reclamation of her people's lost voyaging heritage, arguing that a woman’s "True North" is found not in a partner, but in the integration of her ancestral past with her individual destiny.

The film’s feminist backbone is its sophisticated treatment of female agency and the "female divine." Moana’s quest is a direct rejection of the domestic safety offered by her father, Chief Tui, and a radical embrace of the "mad" wisdom of her grandmother, Tala. By positioning Grandma Tala as the "village crazy lady" who is actually the keeper of ancestral truth, the film validates elder female knowledge as a bridge to liberation. Furthermore, the relationship between Moana and the demigod Maui (Dwayne Johnson) subverts traditional gender dynamics; Maui is not a savior but a flawed, ego-driven partner who must be taught by Moana how to rediscover his own worth. The film posits that true heroism requires the balance of Maui’s physical "brawn" with Moana’s empathetic "wayfinding" intelligence.

Ecofeminism provides the film’s philosophical climax. The reveal that the lava monster Te Kā is actually the goddess Te Fiti, transformed by the trauma of having her "heart" (her agency/identity) stolen by a man, serves as a powerful metaphor for the patriarchal exploitation of nature. Moana’s resolution is not to defeat the monster through violence, but to recognize the goddess beneath the pain through the sung mantra, "I know who you are." This restorative act suggests that environmental healing is inseparable from the recognition of female autonomy. By returning the heart, Moana does not just save her island; she restores the balance between the human and the divine, between the masculine drive to conquer and the feminine power to nurture.

Ultimately, *Moana* redefined the "Disney formula" for a global, contemporary audience. It proves that a young woman's story can be epic, culturally specific, and commercially massive without the presence of a prince. The film’s legacy lies in its celebration of the "Chief" over the "Princess," replacing the crown with the oar as a symbol of authority. In doing so, it offers a new model of leadership for young audiences—one that is rooted in community, tradition, and the courage to set sail beyond the safe confines of the reef.
