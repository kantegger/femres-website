Published in 1968 by Anne Koedt, "The Myth of the Vaginal Orgasm" is one of the most radical and transformative documents of the second-wave feminist movement. In this essay, Koedt thoroughly re-evalutes the then-dominant Freudian understanding of female sexuality, exposing how "facts" regarding women's physical pleasure were distorted to maintain a male-dominated social structure. The work functions as far more than a medical or psychological treatise; it serves as a highly political manifesto for women to reclaim ownership of their own bodies.

The author, Anne Koedt, was a key member of radical feminist organizations such as New York Radical Women and Redstockings. She believed that the root of women's oppression was deeply embedded not only in social institutions but in the most personal of realms: sexuality. In this work, Koedt used scientific anatomical knowledge combined with feminist political analysis as weapons to directly challenge the false dogmas that had bound women for centuries.

The core of the argument is an indictment of the Freudian dichotomy between the "vaginal orgasm" (defined as a sign of mature womanhood) and the "clitoral orgasm" (dismissed as a vestige of immature girlhood). Koedt asserts as an anatomical fact that the clitoris is the primary and only organ for female sexual response. Despite the fact that the vagina contains few nerve endings for pleasure, Koedt asks why the medical community and society chose to ignore the clitoris and define vaginal climax as "true maturity." Her answer is that this definition served as a powerful ideological tool to subordinate female sexuality to a form convenient for men—heterosexual penetration.

According to Koedt, the clitoris is an organ that provides no economic or reproductive benefit to men; it exists purely for "female pleasure." Consequently, patriarchal society sought to systematically render it invisible or pathological. For women to recognize the clitoris as the source of their pleasure would mean that they do not necessarily "need" men in sexual acts, or that men would have to fundamentally reconsider their techniques and attitudes. This represented a profound threat to male privilege and the control of women within a heterosexual framework.

From a feminist perspective, "The Myth of the Vaginal Orgasm" laid the foundation for the concept of "bodily autonomy." Koedt argued that the right to define how one's body functions and what brings it pleasure belongs to the woman herself, not to male therapists or husbands. Her argument sharply criticized the "sexual revolution" of the time, which she saw as expanding sexual freedom for men while further oppressing women as beings who "must" respond with pleasure. True sexual liberation, she argued, means women being free to define their sexual experiences based on their own anatomical truths.

The book also provides important insights into the intersection of sexuality and power. Koedt pointed out how treating female sexuality as "immature" or "pathological" was closely linked to the low status of women in the public sphere, where they were often labeled as irrational or emotional beings. Oppression in the private realm serves as a foundation to justify social oppression. Therefore, redefining sexual pleasure becomes one of the most basic and radical actions for overturning the power dynamics of society as a whole.

In a historical context, the work played a decisive role in incorporating and theorizing scientific findings—such as those from the physiological studies of Masters and Johnson—into the feminist movement. Koedt’s words spoke to countless women who had suffered in silence, blaming themselves for being "frigid," telling them that it was not their fault but the result of social ignorance and lies. This intellectual liberation created an unprecedented sense of solidarity (sisterhood) among women, leading to self-help groups and independent women's health movements.

Regarding mental health, the book was a challenge to the concept of "normalcy" itself. Koedt exposed how Freudian psychology imposed impossible standards on women and planted unnecessary guilt and anxiety. Gaining a deeper understanding of one's own body is the first step toward building healthy self-confidence and liberating oneself from the expectations of others, particularly men. Koedt’s arguments serve as a precursor to modern discussions on positive body image and sexual wellness.

Upon its release, the essay sparked fierce controversy. Voices from conservative groups, and even from within some feminist circles, claimed it was too radical. However, the truth that Koedt presented—that women's bodies were not designed for male pleasure—became the foundation for the body and pleasure theories in all subsequent feminist and queer theories. Her words continue to provide the intellectual energy to question the power relations inherent in all aspects of sexuality across eras.

Re-reading this work today, while the anatomical facts discussed have largely become common knowledge, one realizes that the power asymmetries surrounding sexuality still persist in different forms. The illusion of the "vaginal orgasm" criticized by Koedt remains persistent in modern pornography and mainstream media representations, continuing to obscure authentic female experiences. "The Myth of the Vaginal Orgasm" is more than a historical document of the past; it is a guidebook for the realities we still must fight today.

In conclusion, Anne Koedt’s essay was the bravest first step toward women reconquering the territory of their own bodies. she urges us not to leave our pleasure to the definitions of others or surrender it to social expectations. To know one's truth and begin to speak it publicly—this simple yet vital act is the key to fundamentally changing the world and securing true freedom.

Koedt concludes her essay with words symbolizing women's thirst for freedom: a declaration of determination to no longer be misled by the lies of others and to listen to the voices of their own bodies. Through this essay, we rediscover how our bodies are full of wonder and are meant to be free. That discovery is the most revolutionary gift that Koedt left for us.
