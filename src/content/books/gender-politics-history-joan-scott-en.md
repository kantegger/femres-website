"Gender and the Politics of History," published by historian Joan Wallach Scott in 1988, fundamentally transformed how the historical profession understands gender. In this work, Scott proposes that gender should not be seen as a simple reflection of biological difference but rather as a "category of knowledge" and a "field of power"—a perspective that laid a solid foundation for feminist historical theory and profoundly influenced the humanities and social sciences.

Scott's central argument challenges traditional historiography's treatment of gender as fixed, natural categories. She points out that historians often uncritically accept "men" and "women" as units of analysis while neglecting the historical constructedness of these categories themselves. Drawing on poststructuralist theory, particularly Foucault's power/knowledge framework and Derrida's deconstruction, Scott proposes that gender is a social relation and discursive practice that is "continually being constructed and reconstructed."

The book's opening essay, "Gender: A Useful Category of Historical Analysis," has become a classic text in feminist theory. In this article, Scott distinguishes between two ways of understanding gender: one treats gender as "social gender"—socially constructed masculinity and femininity; the other treats gender as "gender relations"—the power relations that define and construct these categories. Scott advocates for the latter, arguing that only by understanding gender as a power relation can we truly grasp its historical operations.

Scott's critique of the concept of "experience" is equally groundbreaking. She notes that feminist historians often appeal to "women's experience" as the starting point for historical research, but this approach contains theoretical pitfalls—it presupposes "women" as a unified, stable subject category while overlooking how this category itself is historically constructed. Scott argues that the task of historical research is not simply to "recover" repressed women's voices but to analyze how these voices are produced and understood within specific power relations.

The multiple essays collected in this book demonstrate the application of Scott's theoretical framework. In analyzing French working-class history, she reveals how gender categories shaped the definition of "worker"—historically, "worker" was often assumed to be male, while women's labor was marginalized or made invisible. Scott points out that this gendered definition not only reflected past reality but also participated in constructing that reality, influencing the organization and strategies of labor movements.

Scott's treatment of the question of "difference" also demonstrates her theoretical depth. She critiques approaches that treat "difference" simply as biological difference between men and women, advocating instead for understanding difference as historically constructed, unstable, and multiple. Scott notes that gender difference is never singular but always intersects with class, race, sexuality, and other differences. This understanding of difference provided important resources for subsequent intersectionality theory.

The book's impact on historical methodology was profound. Scott proposes that feminist history should not be content with "adding" women's history to existing historical narratives but should question and challenge the categories and assumptions of these narratives themselves. She advocates a "deconstructive" historical practice that reveals the implicit gender assumptions in historical narratives and analyzes how these assumptions participate in the reproduction of power relations.

Scott's theory sparked extensive discussion and controversy in academic circles. Some critics argued that her poststructuralist position was too abstract and distant from feminist political goals; others noted that her critique of "experience" might undermine the foundation of feminist politics. Yet these criticisms themselves prove the influence of Scott's thought—she forced feminist scholars and historians to reexamine their theoretical premises and political commitments.

From a contemporary perspective, many of Scott's insights have been widely accepted. Gender as a category of analysis, gender relations as power relations, the historical constructedness of gender categories—these views have now become basic consensus in feminist and gender studies. However, Scott's theory also faces new challenges, particularly from transgender studies and non-Western feminisms, which further complicate our understanding of gender, body, and identity.

Scott's legacy lies in her combination of rigorous theoretical thinking with concrete historical practice. She demonstrated that poststructuralist theory can serve feminist politics and that theoretical analysis can enhance rather than weaken the power of political action. In this sense, "Gender and the Politics of History" is not merely a book about history but a book about knowledge, power, and liberation—it invites us to continually question existing categories and imagine different futures.
