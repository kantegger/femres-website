Published in 2017, Angela Saini’s "Inferior: How Science Got Women Wrong - and the New Research That's Rewriting the Story" is a pivotal work that shakes the myth of scientific objectivity. It exposes the deep-seated gender biases hidden behind "scientific" discourses surrounding women's bodies, intellect, and social roles. As a science journalist, Saini navigates through biology, psychology, anthropology, and neuroscience to illustrate how science itself has often—sometimes arbitrarily—concluded that women are "inferior." Through meticulous research and clear logic, she reveals the human prejudices that have historically shaped scientific inquiry.

Angela Saini is a highly acclaimed science journalist with a background in engineering, known for her work with the BBC and other major outlets. Her approach is not to worship science as absolute truth, but to observe it calmly as a "human endeavor" that cannot escape the cultural and temporal biases of its practitioners. In "Inferior," Saini analyzes how data has been interpreted and manipulated to reinforce specific societal narratives, while simultaneously introducing the latest, more equitable research that is finally overturning these ancient misconceptions.

The book begins by tracing the history of misogyny within the scientific community, from Charles Darwin to the present day. Even Darwin, the father of evolution, believed that women were intellectually "inferior" and could never reach the same heights as men in intellectual debate. Saini points out how the personal prejudices of such great scientists were transformed into academic dogma, passed down uncritically through generations of scientists—especially in male-dominated institutions. Science is not merely a collection of numbers; gender dynamics are at play from the very moment a question is formulated.

Saini dives into the frequently debated topic of "brain differences" between genders using the latest findings in neuroscience. For years, the theory of a "male brain" adapted for systems and a "female brain" adapted for empathy has been widely accepted by the public. However, Saini reveals that many of these studies suffer from small sample sizes, overemphasize minute differences, or ignore the vast influence of cultural and educational backgrounds. The latest research she introduces suggests that brains do not fall into a binary "male" or "female" category, but are instead a "brain mosaic" where individual differences far outweigh any statistical gender differences.

Furthermore, the book challenges stereotypes in evolutionary biology, particularly the schema of the "passive female" and the "aggressive male." Many evolutionary models have assumed that men hunted while women remained subservient, focusing solely on gathering and childcare. Saini cites the latest anthropological and archaeological discoveries to show that women played active roles in hunter-gatherer societies, participated in hunting itself, and exerted significant influence on community decision-making. These findings suggest that so-called "traditional gender roles" are often fantasies that modern society has projected onto the past.

The "scientific" misconceptions regarding women's sexuality are also a key subject of Saini’s analysis. The view of women as sexually modest and reticent has been firmly maintained since Darwin’s time. However, through primate research and studies of isolated tribes, Saini reveals just how diverse and active female sexuality truly is. She corrects the historical record where science treated female desire as an "abnormality" or an "exception," using modern biological insights to provide a more accurate picture.

In terms of mental health, Saini examines the stereotype that hormones are the primary cause of women’s "whims" or instability. She highlights how science has historically defined the female body as an "abnormal male body" and pathologized natural processes like menstruation and menopause—a critique that functions as a feminist indictment of the modern medical system. She advocates for the necessity of scientists to view bodily processes objectively, without the lens of historical prejudice.

Throughout the book, Saini emphasizes that for science to truly protect its claim to objectivity, it must include more diverse perspectives, particularly the participation of female scientists. It is difficult for individuals to notice their own biases, but when people from different backgrounds verify the same data, hidden assumptions are exposed, and science can move closer to the truth. Her work serves as a reminder that science is a process that must be constantly corrected and updated.

"Inferior" is not simply a book claiming that "women are as good as men." It is a cultural critique that calmly analyzes how science has been part of power structures and used as a tool to justify the existing social system. Saini provides readers with a powerful intellectual weapon to question "scientific evidence" whenever their own capabilities are doubted.

The book also carries significant implications for education. The unfounded belief that female students are naturally ill-suited for STEM subjects remains persistent around the world. Saini proves that much of this disparity is not biological destiny but is created by social expectations and educational environments. "Inferior" serves as an empowering text for young women, urging them not to let others define their limits.

In conclusion, Angela Saini’s "Inferior" is essential reading for anyone interested in science, feminism, and justice. It is a hopeful, scientific manifesto that deconstructs the "obvious" and redefines feminine potential. Acknowledging that science has historically provided wrong answers about women and rewriting the story through the latest, fairer research is an indispensable step toward building a more equal and just society.

In Saini’s words, true science lies in having the courage to accept facts as they are, even when they contradict one’s beliefs or social conventions. Rewriting the scientific story of women is not just about correcting past errors; it is about creating a future where all humans can realize their true potential, unbound by the constraints of gender. "Inferior" serves as a reliable map toward that future.
