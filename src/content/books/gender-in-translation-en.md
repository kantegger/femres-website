Sherry Simon's *Gender in Translation: Cultural Identity and the Politics of Transmission* (1996) is widely recognized as one of the founding texts of feminist translation studies, a field that examines the complex intersections of gender, language, and the politics of cultural transfer. Before Simon's intervention, translation studies was dominated by paradigms of "fidelity" and "equivalence" that imagined translation as a neutral, almost mechanical transfer of meaning from one language to another. Simon, drawing on feminist theory, deconstructed these metaphors and revealed the profoundly gendered assumptions hidden within them. The traditional language of translation, she argued, is shot through with patriarchal binaries: the "original" is cast as the masculine author/father, while the "translation" is its feminine, secondary, and derivative copy—a "handmaiden" to the master text. By exposing this hierarchy, Simon opened a new field of inquiry into how gender shapes the very concepts we use to think about language, authorship, and interpretation.

Simon's central argument is that translation is not a transparent window onto an original text but a productive act of rewriting. Every translation involves choices—about vocabulary, syntax, tone, and cultural reference—that are shaped by the translator's identity, including their gender. When a woman translates a text, she brings to it a specific historical experience and perspective that was often invisible or devalued in traditional translation practice. Simon examines the work of several pioneering feminist translators, including the Canadian scholars who translated works of Québécois feminist writers. These translators did not aim for a neutral, self-effacing "fidelity" to the source text; instead, they actively intervened, using strategies like neologisms, wordplay, and supplementary footnotes to foreground feminist meanings that might otherwise be lost in translation. This practice, sometimes called "hijacking" or "womanhandling" the text, was a political act of making women's voices audible across linguistic and cultural borders.

The book also explores the historical erasure of women from the annals of translation. Throughout history, translation has been one of the few literary activities open to women, precisely because it was considered a secondary, derivative form of writing rather than "original" creation. Yet this marginalization was a double-edged sword: while it allowed women access to the world of letters, it also obscured their intellectual labor. Simon recovers the stories of women translators who played crucial roles in the transmission of ideas between cultures, from the medieval period through the Enlightenment and into the twentieth century. These women were not merely passive conduits but active agents who shaped the reception of texts in their target cultures, often introducing new ideas that challenged the status quo.

Furthermore, Simon engages with the postcolonial dimensions of translation. She notes that the metaphor of the translator as a "traitor" (traduttore, traditore) has particular resonance for subjects of colonial power, for whom the passage between languages is always fraught with questions of loyalty and identity. Feminist and postcolonial perspectives converge in their critique of translation as a site of power, revealing how the dominant language often erases the specificities of the source culture, particularly the voices of marginalized groups within it. Simon calls for a practice of "thick translation" that attends to these power dynamics, making visible the labor of the translator and the political stakes of their choices.

*Gender in Translation* has had a profound impact on both translation studies and feminist theory. It helped establish feminist translation studies as a recognized subfield, inspiring a generation of scholars to examine how gender inflects the practice of translation across different historical and cultural contexts. It also contributed to the broader "cultural turn" in translation studies, which moved the field away from purely linguistic concerns toward questions of identity, power, and representation. Simon's work reminds us that language is never neutral; it is a battlefield where meanings are contested and identities are forged. In an era of increasing global exchange and linguistic hybridity, her insights into the politics of translation remain more relevant than ever.
