"The Reproduction of Mothering: Psychoanalysis and the Sociology of Gender," published by American sociologist and psychoanalyst Nancy Chodorow in 1978, is a classic feminist work that uses psychoanalytic and sociological theory to explain why women become mothers and how this gender division is psychologically reproduced.

Chodorow's central question is: Why does "mothering" remain almost exclusively women's responsibility in a society that increasingly values gender equality? She rejects simple biological determinism, instead exploring the complex interactions between social structure, family relationships, and psychological development. Chodorow argues that the phenomenon of "women doing mothering" is not a natural product but the result of social and psychological mechanisms working together.

Chodorow's research is based on object relations theory, particularly the theories of Winnicott, Fairbairn, and Margaret Mahler. She proposes that children's early object relations—especially with their primary caregiver (usually the mother)—have a decisive impact on gender identity formation. Girls, being the same gender as their mother, undergo a radically different identification process than boys: girls develop their sense of self through ongoing relationship with their mother, while boys must construct masculine identity through separation from their mother.

Chodorow finds that these different developmental paths lead to different gender personality structures. Girls tend to develop a "relational self"—they value interpersonal relationships more, are better at maintaining emotional connections with others, but are also more prone to merger anxiety with their mother. Boys tend to develop an "independent self"—they emphasize autonomy and separation more, but are also more likely to repress emotional needs and view relationships as domains of power and control.

Chodorow points out that these gender personality differences are not biologically determined but shaped by the social arrangement of "women doing mothering." When mothers are the sole primary caregivers, both girls and boys must negotiate their relationship with their mother in different ways, forming different patterns of gender identity. Once formed, these psychological structures are reproduced in adulthood—women are more inclined to take on mothering because they value relationships more; men are more inclined to avoid care work because they view relationships as threats to autonomy.

Chodorow's critique of Freud's theory of femininity is an important component of this book. She notes that Freud's concept of "penis envy" reflects a male-centered bias—taking the male body as the standard and defining women as "lack." Chodorow offers an alternative understanding: girls' turn from mother to father is not due to envy of the penis but due to seeking relationship with an "other" that is difficult to achieve in the same-sex mother-daughter relationship.

Chodorow's analysis of the "motherhood penalty" was prescient. She notes that mothering is not only a labor burden but also a psychological burden—mothers are expected to provide unconditional love and care for their children, but these expectations often leave mothers feeling repressed and angry. Chodorow calls for society to recognize the value of mothering labor and advocates for shared parenting responsibility to break the gendered cycle of mothering.

Chodorow's legacy lies in her demonstration of how psychological structures are shaped by social relations and how changing social arrangements can change psychological structures. She proved that "women doing mothering" is not a natural necessity but a historical product that can be changed.
