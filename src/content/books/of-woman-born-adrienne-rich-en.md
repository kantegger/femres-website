"Of Woman Born: Motherhood as Experience and Institution," published by American poet and feminist theorist Adrienne Rich in 1976, is a classic feminist work that deeply analyzes how motherhood as an institution is shaped and controlled by patriarchy while exploring the liberating potential inherent in the experience of mothering itself.

Rich makes a crucial distinction in this book: motherhood as "experience" versus motherhood as "institution." Motherhood as experience is the real relationship between women and their children, filled with love, contradiction, creativity, and complexity; while motherhood as institution is a set of norms, expectations, and oppressive mechanisms constructed by patriarchy, which defines women as "mothers" rather than complete individuals, sanctifying motherhood while simultaneously devaluing it.

The first part of the book, "Institution," traces the construction of motherhood in Western history. Rich analyzes the evolution of motherhood concepts from ancient Greece to modern times, revealing how motherhood has been shaped by medicine, religion, law, and state power. She particularly critiques the "male takeover" of childbirth by modern medicine—male obstetricians replacing female midwives, transforming childbirth from a natural, women-led process into a "disease" requiring medical intervention.

Rich offers a profound critique of the myth of "maternal instinct." She notes that so-called "maternal instinct" is an ideological construct used to confine women to the maternal role while masking the political nature of motherhood through its naturalization. Rich cites historical and anthropological evidence showing vast differences in understandings and practices of motherhood across cultures.

The second part of the book, "Experience," explores the complexity of motherhood as a real human experience. Rich writes honestly about her own experiences as a mother—the joy, exhaustion, contradiction, anger, and creativity within it. She refuses to romanticize or demonize motherhood, instead viewing it as an experience filled with tension and possibility.

Rich's analysis of the "motherhood penalty" was prescient. She notes that motherhood causes women to suffer losses in career, economics, and social status, but these losses are culturally viewed as "natural" and unchangeable. Rich calls for the creation of a "motherhood-friendly" society—providing affordable childcare, paid parental leave, flexible work arrangements, and recognizing the economic value of mothering labor.

The book's attention to lesbian mothers and single mothers was ahead of its time. Rich critiques the ideology that defines the "normal" family as a heterosexual two-parent family, noting that this definition not only excludes other forms of family but also reinforces women's subordinate position in traditional family structures.

Rich's legacy lies in bringing motherhood from the margins to the center, proving that motherhood is not only a "women's issue" but a core issue concerning how society organizes care labor and understands human relationships.
