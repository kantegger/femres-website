"The Madwoman in the Attic: The Woman Writer and the Nineteenth-Century Literary Imagination," published by American literary critics Sandra Gilbert and Susan Gubar in 1979, is a classic work of feminist literary criticism. This book presents groundbreaking research on nineteenth-century British and American women's literature, deeply analyzing how women writers sought and constructed their voices within patriarchal literary traditions, and is hailed as a foundational work of feminist literary criticism.

Gilbert and Gubar's central argument is that nineteenth-century women writers faced a unique "anxiety of authorship"—they had to overcome not only the universal difficulties of literary creation but also fight for the right to create in a culture that viewed women as "muses" rather than "creators." They borrow Harold Bloom's concept of "anxiety of influence" but transform it into "anxiety of authorship"—women writers must find their place within a literary tradition that treats male experience as the universal standard and female experience as "other."

The book's analysis of Charlotte Brontë's "Jane Eyre" is particularly famous. Gilbert and Gubar interpret Bertha Mason—the "madwoman" locked in the attic—as the embodiment of Jane Eyre's repressed anger and passion. They note that Bertha is not Jane's opposite but her "dark double"—she does what Jane wants to do but cannot: burning down Thornfield Hall, resisting Rochester's domination. This interpretation reveals a recurring theme in nineteenth-century women's literature: how women writers used the figure of the "madwoman" to express their anger and resistance against patriarchy.

Gilbert and Gubar trace the nineteenth-century women's literary tradition from Jane Austen to Emily Dickinson. They discover that these women writers shared a unique set of themes, images, and narrative strategies—though these expressions were often covert and symbolic. Women writers frequently used images such as "doubles," "madwomen," "illness," and "enclosed spaces" to express their rage at cultural dispossession, their madness and fragmentation under patriarchy, and their rebellion against it.

The book's analysis of Milton's "Paradise Lost" is also significant. Gilbert and Gubar note that Milton's epic had a profound influence on nineteenth-century women writers, but this influence was contradictory—women writers were both attracted to Milton's grand narrative and had to resist the ideology within it that defined women as "subordinate." They analyze how Mary Shelley's "Frankenstein" uniquely responds to Milton, revealing how women writers constructed their literary identities through rewriting classics.

Gilbert and Gubar's analysis of Emily Dickinson is equally profound. They note that Dickinson's poetry is filled with metaphorical resistance to literary patriarchy—her refusal to publish, her choice of a "white life," was both a self-limitation and a self-empowerment. Dickinson's poetic language—its fractured syntax, strange imagery, obsession with death—is interpreted as women's attempt to find modes of expression in a culture that excluded them from public discourse.

The book caused a tremendous response in academic circles upon publication. It was praised for proving the existence of a women's literary tradition and establishing a methodological foundation for feminist literary criticism. However, the book also received some criticism. Scholars have pointed out that Gilbert and Gubar's treatment of the category "woman" is too essentialist, ignoring differences of race, class, and culture; their romanticization of the "madwoman" may obscure the real harm of mental illness to women; their reduction of women's literature to expressions of anger against patriarchy may overlook the diversity of women's literature.

From a contemporary perspective, some arguments in "The Madwoman in the Attic" may require revision. Contemporary feminist literary criticism pays more attention to intersectionality, postcolonial perspectives, and queer theory, offering more complex understandings of the category "woman" itself. However, Gilbert and Gubar's groundbreaking contribution is undeniable—they brought women's literature from the margins to the center, proving that women writers also possess rich literary traditions and unique aesthetic values.

Gilbert and Gubar's legacy lies in their demonstration of how literary criticism can become a tool of feminist politics. They proved that close reading can be combined with ideological critique, and that academic research can serve social change. In this sense, "The Madwoman in the Attic" is not only a work of literary criticism but also a book about knowledge, power, and liberation—it invites us to reexamine those marginalized voices and imagine different literary futures.
