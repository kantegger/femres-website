In *Happening* (*L'Événement*), Nobel laureate Annie Ernaux performs what she calls an "ethnological study of herself," transforming a deeply personal trauma into a clinical, universal dissection of memory and repression. The year is 1963, and in a pre-1975 France where abortion is not just illegal but a crime punishable by imprisonment—for both the woman and anyone who assists her—twenty-three-year-old university student Annie finds herself pregnant. For Ernaux, seeking to escape her working-class roots through education, this pregnancy is not merely a "problem"—it is a social death sentence that threatens to hurl her back into the world of manual labor and domestic drudgery she has fought so hard to transcend. To become a mother now would mean abandoning her studies, marrying a man she does not love (or being forever shamed as an unwed mother), and replicating the exhausted, narrow life of her own mother. The fetus inside her becomes, in her stark formulation, "the enemy of my future."

The narrative unfolds as a desperate, almost thriller-like quest for a solution in a world of deafening silence. Abortion is a word that cannot be spoken; it exists only in whispers, in knowing glances, in cryptic suggestions. Ernaux vividly describes the hostility of the medical establishment: doctors who look at her with contempt or refuse to meet her eyes, some even prescribing drugs under false pretenses meant to "prevent miscarriage" that actually strengthen the fetus. She learns that the system is designed not to help women like her, but to punish them—to force them to carry unwanted pregnancies to term as a kind of moral correction. She is encased in a solitude so absolute it becomes physical. Her boyfriend is useless, vanishing at the first sign of trouble; her friends are distant, unwilling to risk complicity; and her family must not know, for the shame would be unbearable. The "event" becomes a wedge separating her from the rest of humanity, locking her into a body that has become her prison.

Ernaux famously writes, "I have finished with the world of the law." This sentence encapsulates the book's radical project: to speak the unspeakable. She refuses to shroud the experience in euphemism or sentimentality. There is no moralizing, no self-pity, and crucially, no apology. Instead, she documents the physical reality of the illegal abortion with a brutal precision that forces the reader to look, to witness. She details the terrifying meeting with the "faiseuse d'anges" (the "angel-maker," a back-alley abortionist) in a cramped kitchen in Paris—an older woman who performs the procedure for a fee, coldly and efficiently, as if she were hemming a dress. Ernaux describes the insertion of the probe, the cramping, and the agonizing days of waiting for the expulsion, during which she continues to attend classes, pretending that nothing is happening.

The climactic scene in her university dormitory restroom is written with a visceral intensity that is almost unbearable, yet necessary. Alone in the middle of the night, she expels the fetus, kneeling on cold tiles, and must decide what to do with the physical remains. She describes it all: the blood, the cord, the small form. There is no redemption in this scene, no catharsis—only the brute facticity of a body in crisis and a young woman utterly alone. By transforming the private shame of the "event" into a public record, Ernaux reclaims her body from the state and the church. She refuses the silence that was meant to erase her experience.

The book is also a profound meditation on time and writing. Written nearly forty years after the event, Ernaux constantly moves between the terrified girl of 1963 and the established writer of 2000 who is trying to reconstruct the truth from the fog of memory. She investigates her own diary entries from that time—sparse, evasive, coded—analyzing the gaps and the euphemisms she used to hide the truth even from herself. The diary says "nothing happened" on the day of the abortion. Ernaux the writer knows that this absence is itself a text, a record of what could not be said. This temporal distance allows her to analyze her younger self not just as a victim, but as a product of her specific class and historical moment. The young Annie was not simply unlucky; she was trapped by a system that criminalized female sexuality and reserved bodily autonomy for the wealthy.

She starkly illustrates how the ban on abortion was a class weapon. Wealthy women could travel to Switzerland or England, where discreet clinics catered to those who could pay. Working-class women like her were left to risk their lives with amateur procedures, knitting needles, and back-alley abortionists. The criminalization of abortion was never about morality; it was about control—about ensuring that poor women remained poor, that class lines were not crossed. Ernaux's trajectory—the working-class girl who defied her fate to become a celebrated author—was only possible because she survived. Many did not.

The book refuses to be only about abortion. It is about the body as a site of political struggle, about memory as a form of resistance, about the ways in which silence is imposed on those who suffer. Ernaux's writing strips away the comforting narratives that society tells about itself. There is no villain in her story except the system—the laws, the doctors, the silence of an entire culture complicit in the subjugation of women.

Ultimately, *Happening* is an act of resistance against forgetting. Ernaux argues that if this experience is not written down, it effectively never happened, and the suffering of countless women remains erased from history. The book is her refusal to let that happen. In an era where reproductive rights are once again under siege globally—from Poland to the United States to Latin America—*Happening* stands as a chilling, essential reminder of what is at stake. Not abstract rights, but the visceral, bloody reality of women's survival and dignity. It is a book that insists on being read, and on never being forgotten.
