Adrienne Rich's 1980 essay *Compulsory Heterosexuality and Lesbian Existence* is one of the most influential and controversial pieces of feminist theory ever written, a text that fundamentally reshaped the relationship between feminism and queer politics. Originally published in the feminist journal *Signs*, the essay posed a radical question that most feminist theorists of the time had failed to ask: Why is heterosexuality assumed to be natural, innate, and the unquestioned default? Rich argued that what appears to be a personal choice or biological drive is, in fact, a political institution—a system of "compulsory heterosexuality" enforced through violence, economic dependence, and ideological control to ensure male access to women's bodies, labor, and emotional support.

Rich begins by critiquing mainstream feminist scholarship for its "lesbian blindness"—its failure to even consider lesbianism as a possibility. She notes that feminist academics of the era, when discussing women's lives and sexuality, consistently framed their analysis around relationships with men, treating heterosexuality as a given rather than as a phenomenon requiring explanation. This erasure, Rich argues, is itself a symptom of the very institution she seeks to expose. By not questioning why women "choose" men, feminism inadvertently reinforces the heterosexual matrix. Rich's challenge was thus directed not only at patriarchal society but at the feminist movement itself, urging it to confront its own heteronormative assumptions.

The essay's central concept is "compulsory heterosexuality." Drawing on a wide range of evidence—from clitoridectomy and footbinding to sexual harassment and the economic structures that make marriage a survival strategy—Rich demonstrates that heterosexuality is not a natural outcome of female desire but a system of control. She invokes historian Kathleen Grier's observation that the measures used to enforce female heterosexuality resemble those used to control colonized populations, suggesting that women under patriarchy are subject to a form of internal colonization. The "choice" of heterosexuality, Rich argues, is no choice at all when all other paths are violently foreclosed.

To counter the erasure of lesbian existence, Rich introduces the concept of the "lesbian continuum." This term describes a broad range of "woman-identified experience"—from genital sexuality between women to all forms of intense female bonding and resistance to patriarchy, including women who have historically lived independently of men, such as spinsters, women in religious orders, or participants in feminist movements. Rich argues that by recognizing this continuum, we can restore visibility to the vast and varied history of female resistance and solidarity that has been deliberately obscured. This concept was both celebrated and criticized: celebrated for creating a space for female solidarity that extended beyond sexual identity, and criticized for potentially desexualizing lesbian identity by subsuming it under a broader category of female friendship.

The essay is fundamentally a work of feminist political theory, not just queer theory. Rich's primary concern is the liberation of all women, and she sees compulsory heterosexuality as a cornerstone of patriarchal control. By forcing women into relationships with men, the system secures men's access to women's reproductive labor, domestic service, and emotional care. It also divides women from one another, preventing the kind of solidarity that could threaten male power. Lesbianism, for Rich, is thus not merely a sexual orientation but a form of political resistance—an "act of resistance" against patriarchy. This framing was powerful but also controversial, as some lesbians objected to having their identities defined primarily by their political utility to feminism.

*Compulsory Heterosexuality and Lesbian Existence* has been subject to significant critique, particularly from intersectional and poststructuralist perspectives. Critics have noted that Rich's essay, while groundbreaking, tends to present "women" as a monolithic category, underplaying how race, class, and colonialism shape experiences of sexuality and gender. Later queer theorists, like Judith Butler, would build on Rich's insights while complicating her framework, arguing that even the categories of "lesbian" and "woman" are themselves effects of power that require deconstruction. Nevertheless, Rich's essay remains a foundational text. It was one of the first to explicitly name and challenge the institution of heterosexuality, opening a door that subsequent queer and feminist theorists would walk through. Its core insight—that what we take to be natural desire is shaped by political and economic structures—remains as vital today as it was in 1980.
