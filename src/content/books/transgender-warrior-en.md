Published in 1996, Leslie Feinberg’s "Transgender Warrior: Making History from Joan of Arc to Dennis Rodman" is a vital and moving work that repaints transgender history from a "history of the dispossessed" into a "history of proud warriors." While monologuing about her own difficult upbringing as a transgender person, Feinberg mobilizes knowledge from history, anthropology, and sociology to bring to light how gender diversity has existed since the dawn of civilization and how it has been systematically suppressed by later power structures.

The author, Leslie Feinberg, is also known as the author of the novel "Stone Butch Blues." Her approach is characterized by a materialist view of history, considering the oppression of gender as closely linked to the establishment of private property and class society. In this work, she goes beyond mere historical introduction, seeking to teach roots and provide intellectual weapons for all transgender people currently facing discrimination and violence.

The narrative begins with Feinberg’s own personal questions: "Who am I?" and "Why does society hate me so much?". To answer these urgent questions, she dives into the depths of history. There she discovers that in many indigenous societies and ancient civilizations, people who crossed the boundaries of the two genders were even objects of veneration as shamans, prophets, and protectors. Feinberg proves that gender dualism (man or woman) is not a universal truth but a historical product constructed for specific political and economic interests.

One of the highlights of the book is the reinterpretation of Saint Joan of Arc. Feinberg highlights the aspect that one of the true reasons Joan was executed was her steadfast refusal to stop wearing men's clothes—namely, gender crossing itself. By placing Joan in the lineage of "transgender warriors," she clarifies that Joan's fight was not a mere war between nations but a struggle for the right to define one's own body and identity.

From a feminist perspective, "Transgender Warrior" greatly expands the scope of "bodily autonomy." Feinberg points out that the oppression of women and the oppression of transgender people stem from the same root of "fixing and managing roles by sex." She argues against the exclusion of transgender people by the feminist movement, asserting that the right for all people to freely express their own bodies and genders is the foundation of true liberation. The importance of intersectional solidarity is powerfully advocated here as well.

The book is also a powerful critique of Western-centric views of history. The wide array of cases regarding "third genders" and gender-crossing rituals in indigenous cultures of Asia, Africa, and the Americas fundamentally shakes the reader's narrow gender views. Feinberg indicts the process through which colonialism violently destroyed local diverse gender cultures and imposed Christian moral views.

In a historical context, the Stonewall riots of 1969 are also handled in detail. Feinberg re-evaluates the fact that the people who fought the police on the front lines in this riot, which became the starting point for the modern LGBTQ+ movement, were often transgender women and drag queens who had been erased from history. They were indeed modern "warriors," and without their courage, there would be no road to the acquisition of rights today.

From a mental health perspective, the book serves as "self-affirmation therapy." For people who have been labeled "abnormal" or "pathological" by society, knowing that they have thousands of years of history and ancestors who were respected in great civilizations provides immeasurable salvation. Feinberg's words continue to send a powerful message to young people in loneliness: "You are never alone, and your existence is historically correct."

The abundant use of illustrations is also a major feature of the work. Feinberg's consideration in trying to facilitate a visual understanding of gender diversity through sculptures, paintings, and photographs from around the world conveys "the truth of existence" beyond words. It is filled with the figures of people living diverse genders—sometimes beautiful, sometimes strict, and sometimes humorous.

Since its publication, "Transgender Warrior" has not only contributed to the academic development of the field but has become a "bible" for many activists around the world. Feinberg’s project of reading the history of oppression into a history of courage was subsequently carried on by many researchers and writers, becoming the driving force for opening the new horizon of queer history.

Furthermore, the book consistently maintains a working-class perspective. Feinberg defines transgender rights as matters of employment rights, housing rights, and basic survival rights. For her, the liberation of gender was inseparable from liberation from capitalist exploitation. This thorough realism makes her arguments urgent and rooted in life, rather than mere abstract discussion.

In conclusion, Leslie Feinberg’s work is a magnificent hymn to generations of people who have been silenced. It teaches readers how difficult and how noble it is to live by upholding one's own truth. A warrior is not one who holds a gun. It is one who takes pride in their identity and continues to say "NO" to an absurd reality for the sake of others and for the future.

Finally, Feinberg explains the importance of solidarity to the warriors who will lead the future. "By knowing our past, we can win our future." Those words filled with conviction continue to beat powerfully in the hearts of readers—from those at the front lines of protests to a single reader quietly opening the book in their room. The torch of history that Leslie Feinberg lit continues to illuminate the darkness and lead us toward the completion of a freer, more beautiful humanity.
