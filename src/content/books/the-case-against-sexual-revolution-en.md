*The Case Against the Sexual Revolution* is a highly controversial work published in 2022 by British journalist and women's rights activist Louise Perry. From the perspective of a self-described "liberal feminist," the book offers a sharp critique of the sexual revolution since the 1960s, advocating for a return to more traditional sexual ethics to protect women's interests.

Perry's central argument is that the sexual revolution promised women freedom and liberation, but in reality has left many women—particularly working-class women—in a more vulnerable position. She argues that dating apps, hookup culture, the proliferation of pornography, and the legalization of sex work have superficially expanded women's "choices" while actually reinforcing men's sexual exploitation of women.

The book's structure revolves around six "lies" that Perry believes form the foundation of contemporary sexual culture:

The first lie is that "men and women are the same when it comes to sex." Perry cites evolutionary psychology and sociological research showing that men are more accepting of casual sexual relationships than women, while women tend to seek sexual fulfillment within committed relationships. When culture encourages women to mimic men's sexual behavior patterns, it is actually asking women to suppress their authentic desires.

The second lie is that "sex work is just work." Perry interviewed numerous former sex workers, documenting their trauma and exploitation. She argues that legalizing sex work cannot eliminate its inherent violence, because the essence of sex work is using money to purchase temporary ownership of another person's body, which contradicts the core feminist principle of bodily autonomy.

The third lie is that "pornography is harmless entertainment." Perry analyzes the content of mainstream pornography in detail, pointing out that it is filled with violence, humiliation, and objectification of women. She argues that pornography not only reflects societal misogyny but actively produces and spreads it, particularly affecting young men's sexual attitudes.

The fourth lie is that "we can separate sex from love." Perry cites neuroscience research showing that sex releases oxytocin and other "bonding hormones," creating emotional connections in the human brain. When culture encourages "no-strings-attached sex," it is actually asking humans to go against their biological nature.

The fifth lie is that "marriage is an oppressive institution." Perry acknowledges that traditional marriage did oppress women, but she argues that abandoning the institution of marriage has not liberated women; instead, it has left many women—particularly single mothers—in economic hardship. She advocates reforming the institution of marriage rather than abolishing it.

The sixth lie is that "women should pursue careers like men." Perry points out that when society demands women meet the same professional standards as men while still bearing most of the responsibility for housework and childcare, it is actually asking women to work "two full-time jobs." She argues that we should reevaluate the value of "work" and "family" rather than simply encouraging women to "lean in."

Perry's arguments have sparked fierce debate in feminist circles. Critics accuse her of being a "pseudo-feminist," defending patriarchy, and "victim-blaming." They argue that Perry portrays women as passive victims of sex, ignoring women's agency and the genuine liberation the sexual revolution has brought to many women.

But Perry insists that her position is the true feminism—one that cares about women's actual well-being rather than abstract "freedom." She writes: "If a 'liberation' makes women more unhappy, more insecure, and more lonely, is it really liberation?"

Whether readers agree with Perry's conclusions or not, *The Case Against the Sexual Revolution* is a book that cannot be ignored. It forces us to confront uncomfortable questions: Has the sexual revolution really made women happier? Who gets to define "freedom" and "liberation"? When women's "choices" conflict with their long-term interests, whose side should feminism be on?

The value of this book lies not in providing all the correct answers, but in daring to ask questions deemed "politically incorrect" by contemporary culture. In a public discourse increasingly prone to camp-making and self-censorship, this intellectual courage is itself a contribution.
