Published in 2020, Cathy Park Hong’s "Minor Feelings: An Asian American Reckoning" is a provocative and profound collection of essays that sent shockwaves through the American discourse on race, identity, and power. A poet by trade, Hong uses her personal experiences to name and analyze the "minor feelings" peculiar to the Asian American experience—emotions that are not merely individual grievances but the inevitable byproduct of a racial hierarchy that marginalizes Asian voices while superficially praising them.

Cathy Park Hong, an acclaimed poet known for works like "Engine Empire," brings a Korean American perspective that serves as a powerful engine for blending lyrical metaphor with cold, hard social analysis. The "minor feelings" she describes consist of the trapped anger, sadness, and shame that arise when one’s reality is persistently denied and minimized by the dominant society. These feelings are often excluded from mainstream narratives, and individuals are frequently conditioned to believe their experiences "aren't a big deal," even to themselves.

At the heart of the book is a thorough dismantling of the "model minority" myth. Hong argues that while Asian Americans are often upheld by stereotypes of being disciplined, successful, and compliant, this portrayal actually functions as a tool to drive a wedge between them and other minority communities—particularly Black communities—while simultaneously silencing their political and emotional voices. She candidly depicts how this myth has planted deep seeds of self-hatred and a profound sense of alienation within many Asian Americans, who find themselves eternally outsiders in a white-centric society.

Hong's narrative covers a vast cultural landscape, ranging from the stand-up comedy of Richard Pryor to the experimental literature of Theresa Hak Kyung Cha, and the complex relationships with her college friends. She critiques how the Asian body is treated as a "useful but invisible" object or forced into specific narratives like the overachieving prodigy or the passive victim. Her perspective as a poet constantly raises thrilling questions about how language can trap humans into categories—and how it can also be the tool for their liberation.

From a feminist perspective, "Minor Feelings" is a masterpiece of intersectional analysis. Hong reveals how the gendered oppression faced by Asian women is inextricably linked to racial oppression. Their voices are stifled by expectations of being "quiet and submissive," and their bodies are fetishized through Orientalist fantasies. Hong's process of affirming her internal "anger" and honing it into an intellectual weapon serves as a powerful narrative of empowerment for all marginalized women.

The theme of bodily autonomy also flows through the book. Hong explores how a person’s body can be defined as "something other than oneself" in an environment where their words are not understood or correctly recognized. She describes the struggle to reclaim her own sense of self through language—specifically by re-defining English (often seen as the "language of the other") as her own. For Hong, writing is a physical practice full of pain and joy, aimed at reassembling a dismantled self.

From a mental health perspective, the book offers deep insights into the psychological distress and suppressed emotions that are often taboo within Asian communities. The social expectation that "Asians do not suffer" or "hide suffering through hard work" acts as a profound mental strain. By sharing her experiences with therapy and discussing how the "paranoia" of constantly doubting oneself is a direct byproduct of racism, Hong makes the invisible realities of mental violence visible.

The book also raises sharp questions about the complex and often unfairly polarized relationship between Asian and Black communities in modern America. From the L.A. Riots to the contemporary Black Lives Matter movement, Hong examines how Asian Americans have been caught in the dualistic narrative of "white and black," never fully fitting into either story while experiencing their own unique forms of oppression. She argues that true solidarity requires first looking honestly at one’s own unique position and carefully finding the points of intersection with other oppressed peoples.

Released in 2020, "Minor Feelings" coincided with a period of intensified hate crimes against Asian Americans during the COVID-19 pandemic. Hong’s words arrived as a saving message to many who were trembling with fear and helplessness, telling them, "the pain you feel is real." The book not only entered bestseller lists such as those of the Sunday Times but also won the National Book Critics Circle Award, establishing a new standard for modern Asian American literature.

Hong's style is at times self-deprecating, at times fierce, yet always backed by a precise intelligence. She refuses to offer the reader comfortable reconciliation or easy answers. Instead, she demands that they remain within the discomfort and contradictions to face the truth. She understands that this "unease" is the very engine for fundamental social change.

In conclusion, Cathy Park Hong’s memoir is more than a "Bible" for Asian Americans; it is essential reading for everyone living in a racialized society. It is a manifesto for rejecting a life balanced on self-erasure and instead asserting a "genuine self" that is messy, filled with anger, and yet cannot be taken by anyone. Hong’s voice pours new ink into the historical void of silence, vividly mapping out the stories that have remained untold across generations.
