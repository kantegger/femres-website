Published in 2015, Gloria Steinem’s "My Life on the Road" is a memoir of rare insight and empathy from an iconic figure who has led the American feminist movement for decades. Rather than a standard chronological biography, the book uses the lens of "the road"—where Steinem has spent the vast majority of her life—to powerfully illustrate how placing oneself in different communities and listening to others' voices can change individual consciousness and serve as the engine for social transformation. For Steinem, travel has never been about reaching a destination; it is a process found within the movement itself, a way to find truth through dialogue with the people she encounters.

The author, Gloria Steinem, has contributed to the advancement of women's rights through numerous activities since the late 1960s, including the founding of "Ms." magazine, a symbol of second-wave feminism. While she was once primarily seen as a cool, intellectual activist, readers of this memoir learn of her "rootless" childhood and how it laid the foundation for her unique organizing method centered on the power of listening. Her narrative voice is remarkably humble, consistently choosing to shine a light on the brilliance of others rather than herself.

At the root of the story is the influence of her father, Leo Steinem. An antiques dealer who disliked settling down, he loved a life spent driving across America year-round. For young Gloria, "home" was not a place enclosed by walls but the interior of a constantly moving car. This unstable but free childhood planted a heart that does not fear change and a deep curiosity for unknown people. Steinem reflects on how this spirit of travel inherited from her father sustained her throughout her life, providing the energy to cross existing boundaries with ease.

One of the core themes of the book is dialogue as a "circle." Steinem has always valued small "listening circles"—where people sit in a ring and share their experiences—over one-sided speeches in large halls. She argues that the true practice of feminism begins not by imposing theory, but by women starting to tell their own stories in their own words and listening to one another. An airport seat, a diner along the way, or an Indigenous assembly—every place served as her classroom, and the "unexpected conversations" found there enriched her thought.

From a feminist perspective, the memoir embodies intersectionality. Through her travels across the United States, Steinem constantly questioned and expanded her own white, middle-class perspective through deep interactions with African American women, Indigenous leaders, and working-class women. She depicts how the struggle for women's rights is inseparable from racial justice, environmental issues, and Indigenous sovereignty based on her own experiences. In particular, the wisdom she learned regarding decision-making processes and gender roles from Indigenous tribes has become the core of her feminism.

Furthermore, the book redefines the meaning of political "organizing." For Steinem, politics is not something that happens in closed rooms in Washington, D.C., but is shaped on the "road" through the accumulation of grassroots dialogues. She records with a journalistic eye and an activist’s passion how individual voices gather to become a great social swell as she travels through election campaigns, protests, and countless meetings. Her journey is a testimony to the process through which people marginalized from power rediscover their own strength.

Steinem’s evolving feelings toward "home" are another moving aspect of the work. After decades of traveling and staying in hotels or the homes of others, her process of eventually arranging her own residence and accepting the concept of "settling down" later in life reflects her maturity as a woman. She discovers that traveling and putting down roots are not contradictory; a true home exists within the connections to the people one loves and trusts, and those connections are made stronger through the journey.

From a psychological perspective, the work suggests the healing power of "listening." Steinem explains how listening seriously to another's story provides deep validation for the speaker, while simultaneously serving as a process for the listener to overcome their own prejudices and loneliness. Her travel is not just physical movement, but a record of a "movement of the heart" that keeps one open to others. Without explicitly using the term "mental health," the power of dialogue she describes holds the potential to heal the isolation felt by many in the modern world.

Since its publication, "My Life on the Road" has inspired readers worldwide and received much acclaim. Beyond being the memoir of a famous activist, it is highly regarded as a guide for coexistence—for how to connect with people living in worlds different from one's own and expand the circle of empathy. Encountering Steinem’s warm yet sharp intelligence provides a valuable opportunity for readers to reconsider the nature of their own life journeys.

The book is also peppered with stories of countless unsung heroes Steinem met: taxi drivers, flight attendants, students, and unnamed homemakers. She gathers their words and celebrates the true wisdom found there. Their voices were the fuel that sustained her movement and the reason she continued to travel. Her humble style reaffirms that feminism is not for any specific group, but is a journey toward freedom for all people.

In conclusion, Gloria Steinem’s work is an "invitation to solidarity" addressed to those living in the 21st century. She urges us to leave our comfort zones and go out on the road. This does not necessarily mean going far away, but starts with trying to listen to the person sitting next to us and lending an ear to opinions different from our own. What Steinem proved through her long journey is that dialogue is the most steady and powerful weapon for creating a world without violence.

Gloria Steinem continues her journey today. Her story sends a message of hope to the generations creating the future: "the road is still continuing." After finishing this beautiful memoir, we gain the courage to stand on our own "road" and start a dialogue with someone new. Steinem’s record of travel lights a fire of empathy in our hearts that will never go out, inviting us to a journey toward making the world a more just and warm place.
