"Powers of Horror: An Essay on Abjection," published in 1980 by Bulgarian-French theorist and psychoanalyst Julia Kristeva, is a significant work that deeply explores the core concept of "abjection." Analyzing how humans construct subjectivity through the expulsion of the "other," this book has profoundly influenced feminist theory, psychoanalysis, and cultural studies. Kristeva's theory integrates psychoanalysis, linguistics, literary criticism, and philosophy, demonstrating the unique appeal of interdisciplinary thought.

Kristeva's definition of "abjection" is the book's most original contribution. She notes that abjection is a state that is neither subject nor object—something that must be expelled during the formation of the subject. Abject things—such as excrement, blood, corpses—evoke fear and disgust because they blur the boundaries between self and other, subject and object, life and death. Kristeva argues that abjection is what "I" must expel in order to become "I," marking the boundary of subjectivity construction.

The book's analysis of the maternal body is particularly profound. Kristeva points out that the mother's body is the typical site of abjection—during pregnancy, the maternal body blurs the boundary between self and other; during childbirth, the line between life and death becomes uncertain; during breastfeeding, bodily boundaries are penetrated. Kristeva argues that patriarchal culture's rejection and fear of the maternal body serves to maintain the clear boundaries of subjectivity. This analysis provided important theoretical resources for subsequent feminist body politics.

Kristeva's theoretical framework integrates multiple intellectual traditions. She draws on Lacanian psychoanalytic theory, particularly concepts of the mirror stage and the symbolic order; she absorbs Foucault's analysis of power and discourse; she also employs structuralist linguistic methods. However, Kristeva does not simply synthesize these theories but creatively transforms them through the concept of "abjection." She pays special attention to the "pre-Oedipal" phase suppressed in Lacanian theory—the pre-linguistic, pre-symbolic realm of the mother-infant relationship.

The book's analysis of literature and art demonstrates the applied value of Kristeva's theory. She analyzes various texts from ancient Greek tragedy to modernist literature, revealing how themes of abjection are expressed and repressed in different cultural contexts. Her analyses of writers such as Louis-Ferdinand Céline and Georges Bataille are particularly deep, showing how abjection is aestheticized in literature while maintaining its subversive power. Kristeva argues that true art does not simply represent abjection but transforms it through formal innovation, making it a force that challenges existing orders.

Kristeva's analysis of religion and taboo is also valuable. She notes that religious rituals—particularly regulations concerning purity and impurity—function to manage abjection and maintain the boundaries of social order. From Jewish dietary laws to Hindu caste systems, from Christian communion to modern hygiene standards, abjection management has always been an important site of power operation. Kristeva's analysis reveals the cultural and political construction behind these seemingly "natural" disgust responses.

The book's influence on feminist theory is multifaceted. On one hand, Kristeva's attention to the maternal body provided feminist tools for rethinking maternity, childbirth, and bodily experience; on the other hand, her deconstruction of the category "woman"—showing how this category itself is constructed within the symbolic order—inspired subsequent anti-essentialist feminism. However, Kristeva's relationship with feminism is complex—she does not always identify with the label "feminist," and some of her views (such as her emphasis on biological difference) diverge from mainstream feminism.

Kristeva's theory has been widely applied in contemporary cultural studies. The concept of "abjection" has been used to analyze racism, xenophobia, fear of disease and disability, exploitation of animals, and other phenomena. Scholars note that these forms of exclusion follow similar logics: by marking certain groups or beings as "abject," mainstream society constructs and consolidates its own subjectivity. Kristeva's theory provides a powerful analytical framework for understanding these mechanisms of exclusion.

From a contemporary perspective, some of Kristeva's views may require revision. Her association of "woman" with "maternity" may be too essentialist; her romanticization of the pre-Oedipal phase may overlook power relations within it; her focus on culture and language may underestimate the agency of the material body. However, these limitations should not obscure Kristeva's groundbreaking contribution—she extended psychoanalysis from the individual to the cultural level, expanded linguistics from structural analysis to subject formation analysis, and provided a new paradigm for understanding the relationships among subjectivity, body, and culture.

Kristeva's legacy lies in her demonstration of how theoretical thinking can reveal dimensions repressed in everyday life. She proved that disgust and fear are not merely "natural" responses but culturally constructed emotions with political significance. In this sense, "Powers of Horror" is not only a theoretical work but a philosophical meditation on exclusion and inclusion, boundaries and transgression, self and other—it invites us to examine those repressed, expelled, marked-as-"abject" existences and to consider the possibility of a more inclusive form of subjectivity.
