Seven years after the #MeToo movement first exploded into global public consciousness, the latest 2024 report from Tulane University's Newcomb Institute brings a series of deeply disturbing findings: sexual harassment and assault remain an epidemic in American society. Despite widespread public awareness and significant legal reforms, millions of individuals continue to experience sexual violence, particularly in professional environments. The report, which surveyed over 3,300 U.S. adults, paints a grim picture: 82% of women and 42% of men have experienced some form of sexual harassment or assault in their lifetime. Even more shocking is the finding that in the past year alone, 26% of U.S. adults—symbolizing over 68 million people—experienced such violence. These numbers demonstrate that while #MeToo succeeded in starting a conversation, it has yet to fundamentally bend the curve of sexual violence.

The workplace remains a particularly persistent danger zone, with 37% of women reporting experiences of sexual harassment or assault in their professional lives. This harassment manifests in various forms, from inappropriate sexual comments and unwelcome physical contact to more severe instances of sexual coercion and the creation of hostile work environments. The risk is not distributed evenly; female workers in the service industry face the highest risks, while the tech and academic sectors continue to struggle with deep-seated power imbalances that protect perpetrators. The report also highlights the critical importance of an intersectional approach, noting that women of color, LGBTQ+ individuals, and low-income workers face compounded risks. Transgender individuals, for example, are twice as likely as cisgender individuals to experience sexual violence, and Native American women continue to face the highest rates of sexual assault in the country.

While the statistics are discouraging, the #MeToo movement has undoubtedly sparked significant structural changes. Since 2017, nineteen states have enacted new workplace harassment protections, and the federal government has introduced the Speak Out Act and the Ending Forced Arbitration Act, both of which provide survivors with better paths toward justice. Culturally, there has been a notable shift toward believing survivors and prioritizing anti-harassment training within organizations. However, these gains are met with ongoing challenges, including remarkably low reporting rates—only 15% of workplace harassment is ever formally reported—largely due to a justified fear of professional retaliation. Furthermore, a cultural backlash has emerged, with some men avoiding interactions with female colleagues and the discourse of "cancel culture" being used to undermine genuine efforts at accountability.

The digital age has introduced a new frontier of sexual violence that the original movement could scarcely have anticipated. Online spaces have become hotbeds for cyberstalking, which now affects one in four women, as well as the non-consensual sharing of intimate images and the rise of deepfake technology used for sexual exploitation. This global epidemic is mirrored in movements across the world, from South Korea's legal reforms and Japan's Flower Demos to the anti-femicide protests in Latin America. Each region faces its own unique cultural obstacles, yet the underlying theme of systemic power abuse remains constant. Effective prevention requires not just policy changes but a fundamental transformation of organizational and societal culture, including comprehensive consent education in schools and a more active role for men as allies who challenge toxic masculinity.

Ultimately, the 2024 #MeToo report characterizes the current moment as an "unfinished revolution." The fact that one in four American adults experienced sexual violence in the past year is nothing short of a national emergency that demands sustained action beyond social media hashtags. Real change requires a commitment to creating a world where sexual violence is neither tolerated nor ignored, a goal that can only be achieved through legal reform, corporate accountability, and a collective shift in societal norms. While the path to such a world is long, the growing strength of survivor networks and the commitment of younger generations to gender equality offer reason for hope. #MeToo is not a moment in time, but a necessary and ongoing movement until every individual can live with safety, dignity, and bodily autonomy.
